Frequently Asked Questions

These questions are designed to assist you to decide whether or not to submit a proposal to speak at Linux.conf.au 2008. We get hundreds of submissions each year, so you need to know your topic really well before submitting your proposal. If you record yourself presenting your talk, you can get a good idea of how long you'll need for the presentation. Also, by showing it to friends or colleagues, you can get good feedback about improvements that you may be able to make.

Submissions

Q. What information do I need to provide with my submission?

A: Your submission should contain:

  • name
  • abstract
  • brief biography
  • project website
  • A short pitch to video - post on youtube or similar, and provide a link.

Make the name of your talk meaningful - this is what people will see when they're deciding which session to attend; If you want an audience, make it count!

Your abstract should outline what you will talk about. Aim for a paragraph which describes your topic, and the key points you will discuss. It doesn't have to be identical to what you will present, but it should be closely related. It will appear on the conference website and in the booklet. If you are successful and invited to speak at linux.conf.au, you will be given an opportunity to edit the abstract before it is published.

Your biography should contain background information about yourself and your experience regarding your presentation topic. It will also appear in the conference booklet.

Q: Who/What is my target audience?

A: The answer to this question really depends on your topic, and how you wish to deliver it. Linux.conf.au attendees range from novices to people with decades of experience in a computing discipline. Presentations can be highly technical, introductory, non-technical: it's up to you, as long as the content is relevant to Linux.conf.au.

Q: What presentation structures are available to me?

A: The linux.conf.au program includes talks or presentations, tutorials, workshops, lightning talks and Birds of a Feather sessions.

Q: How do the main presentations work?

A: In a nutshell, you stand in front of an audience with your accompanying material and talk for your alloted time. It is highly recommended that you allow some time at the end of your presentation for questions.

Q: How do the tutorials work?

A: Should be half-day or full-day presentations in-depth and have extensive notes.

Q: How do the BOFs work?

A: The BOF convenor (generally the person who first writes the topic name on the BOF board) has a set of notes of topics for discussion and moderates the discussion about them. The convenor does not have to be an expert in the topic, but they need to have a general overview of the topic so that they can steer the discussion in the right direction.

Q: How do the lightning talks work?

A: One option is to write the talk notes in a Wiki that is provided (one page per talk). A presentation laptop can be provided to display the wiki pages on the screen for the presentation.

Q: Can I use my own computer?

A: It is possible to use your own laptop, but you must test it first as time taken fiddling with the display will be deducted from the time allotted.

Q: I don't have much public speaking experience...

A: If you don't have any public speaking experience, or don't really feel comfortable giving talks to a large number of people, start off slowly and give a talk at your local LUG meeting. You could also check out organisations like Toastmasters (located worldwide). At LCA, consider giving a talk at a Miniconf, as the audiences are smaller and the atmosphere is less formal. Alternatively, try presenting a lightning talk.

Q: What technology will be available to me

A: Microphone, data projector (1024x768 min). We hope to have whiteboards and document cameras available as well. Internet access should be available as well via 802.11b/g. Any other requirements (space, setup time) should be listed in your submission

Q: What software must I use for my presentation?

A: Something that works. Just keep in mind the output needs to be readable throughout the theatre and that people won't be happy if the presentation software keeps crashing.

Q: What can I show on my presentation slides?

A: Not semi-naked women... use /dev/brain. Err on the side of caution. If it might not be appropriate, don't show it.

Q: How do the audience questions work?

A: Usually, questions are asked at the end of the presentation so put some time aside for them. Other presentations work equally well when questions are asked during the talk, however this can be distracting to some attendees and to some speakers. If you prefer to answer questions at the end of your talk, feel free to tell your audience that you will only take questions at the end of the presentation.

Please try to repeat any questions you are asked, into the microphone, before answering them. Chances are quite a few people won't have heard what was asked.

We recommended that you make yourself available to conference delegates during the week, as some may wish to talk to you about your topic.

Q: Do I need to submit a paper

A: No. You need to submit an abstract and (if it's accepted) give the talk or tutorial.

Travel and Accommodation

Q: Can you pay for my airfare? Will my accommodation be paid for?

A: We have a limited amount of funding set aside for travel and accommodation assistance. Please indicate whether you need travel assistance on the submission form. Keep in mind this might mean your paper is rejected, as we don't have enough funding for everyone. So if you can get your employer to cover your costs, or you can afford to pay your own travel and accommodation, don't ask for travel assistance.

Q: Do I have to pay the conference registration fee if I'm giving a presentation?

A: This depends on the type of sessions.

  • Main conference, tutorial and keynote speakers attend for free for the whole conference.
  • Miniconf and lightning talk presenters must pay to attend.

Q: As a speaker, do I get any discounts?

A. Yes. If you are invited to speak at linux.conf.au you do not have to pay conference registration fees. If you are invited to speak at a mini-conf you are still expected to register for the conference. Mini-confs are community organised streams, and talks are not selected by the linux.conf.au paper selection committee.

Q: Will I receive any payment?

A. No. linux.conf.au does not pay speakers' fees.

Q: What are the visa requirements for entering Australia?

A: At the time of writing (May 2007), the Australian Government Department of Immigration and Citizenship has the following rules in place for tourists and business people :

Short-stay business visas (less than three months): The Business (Short Stay) visa (subclass 456) allows the bona fide business person to make a visit to Australia of up to three months to conduct business, including attending a conference. If you wish to stay longer than three months, you must obtain a long-term business visa.

In many countries, you can obtain a Business (Short Stay) visa at the same time you purchase an airline ticket. Ask for a Business Electronic Travel Authority (ETA) for Australia. Alternatively, you can apply for a short-stay visa on the http://www.eta.immi.gov.au

Tourists (less than three months): For tourists, you must apply for a short-stay visa for up to 3 months. If you wish to stay for longer than three months, you must obtain a long-term visa, for stays between 3 and 6 months.

http://www.immi.gov.au/skilled/business/business-visit-visa-options.htm http://www.immi.gov.au/allforms/visiting_business_short.htm#short

Q: How do I get to the venue/Melbourne CBD from the airport?

A: The committee will arrange transport for speakers from the airport to the venue. This will be organised with you closer to the conf.

Otherwise, you can catch a taxi (around AU$40 one way), the skybus (AU$15 one way, $24 return), or an ordinary bus.
